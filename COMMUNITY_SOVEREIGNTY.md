## WHAT IS COMMUNITY SOVEREIGNTY?

Community Sovereignty is the working name for the political and social philosophy
that guides every project, tool, and organization Darrian builds.

It doesn't fit cleanly into existing labels — and that's intentional.
The closest existing frameworks are described below, but the synthesis is its own thing.

> **Power for the people. By the people. Regardless of race, class, or ethnicity.**
> **Everyone deserves food, shelter, community, growth, knowledge, and power.**
> **The earth protects us when we protect the earth.**
> **No one should need permission to survive.**

## THE INTELLECTUAL LINEAGE

### Communalism / Libertarian Municipalism — Murray Bookchin
Power at the neighborhood and community level — not the nation-state, not the corporation.
Communities governing themselves in voluntary federation with other communities.
The real-world experiment: **Rojava**, the autonomous region in northern Syria, which operates
on these principles today: women's liberation, ecological responsibility, democratic self-governance.

### Ubuntu Philosophy — African Communal Ethics
*"Umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu"* — I am because we are.
Individual dignity is inseparable from collective flourishing.
No one succeeds alone. No one is disposable. No one eats alone.
This is not charity — it is the recognition that individual sovereignty
only exists within a web of mutual relationship.

### Participatory Economics (Parecon) — Michael Albert & Robin Hahnel
Decentralized economic planning by communities, not markets or states.
Work is valued by effort and social contribution, not by ownership of capital.
Economic decisions are made by the people most affected by them.

### Mutualism / Reciprocal Economics — Proudhon, Kropotkin, cooperative tradition
Exchange without exploitation.
Credit and resources available to all without predatory interest.
The cooperative as the economic unit, not the corporation.

### Fourth World / Indigenous Political Philosophy — Vine Deloria Jr., others
Reciprocal relationship with the land: the land provides, we protect.
No coercive hierarchy. No extraction economy.
The community as the base unit of sovereignty — not the individual, not the state.
"Going back to how it was" — not as nostalgia, but as accurate description of
what sustainable human organization actually looks like.

## HOW THIS DIFFERS FROM EXISTING CATEGORIES

| Category | Community Sovereignty position |
|---|---|
| Capitalism | Rejects extraction and coercive hierarchy; supports voluntary exchange |
| Socialism (state) | Rejects centralized state control; power stays at community level |
| Libertarianism | Agrees on individual freedom; rejects atomized individualism without community |
| Liberal progressivism | Agrees on equity; rejects top-down technocratic solutions |
| Conservatism | Agrees on community, land, and rootedness; rejects exclusion and hierarchy |

This is not a centrist compromise. It is a different axis entirely.
It asks: *What structures allow communities to govern themselves with dignity?*

## THE PRACTICE — NOT JUST THE THEORY

Ideology without practice is decoration.

The **langar tradition** — the Sikh practice of providing free community meals with
no means test, no hierarchy, and no distinction of status — is the prototype.
@bookofkaur is building toward this.

The **Peach State Savings** platform is the financial front door:
- Credit building tools for people banks excluded
- Financial literacy delivered in context, not in a classroom
- Automated savings tools built for irregular income patterns
- Business tools at cooperative pricing

The **homelab** is the infrastructure sovereignty demonstration:
- Community tools that don't require corporate permission to run
- Data that stays in the community's hands
- Agents that do labor while community members rest

## THE COMMUNITY SOVEREIGNTY PROJECT

**Working name**: Community Sovereignty Project
**Tagline**: *Power for the people. By the people. Regardless.*

The org doesn't need to form before the practice is real.
Parties and nonprofits are legitimacy structures built on top of existing movements.

**The sequence:**
1. Build the practice (community kitchen, PSS tools, @bookofkaur)
2. Document what works (video, writing, data)
3. Let people who benefit from the practice shape the org
4. The org forms around what's already real

**What this is NOT:**
- A political party competing in elections (yet)
- A 501(c)(3) requiring corporate-style governance (initially)
- A brand or marketing strategy
- Charity (there is a difference between mutual aid and charity)

## ON TECHNOLOGY AND LIBERATION

AI, automation, and code are tools — not ends.

The question is always: *Who controls the tool, and who does it serve?*

A tool built by a corporation to extract user attention and data is not neutral.
A tool built by a community member that runs on community infrastructure,
serves community needs, and is governed by community decisions — that is different.

The homelab running on CT100 is not a vanity project.
It is a demonstration that communities can own their own infrastructure.

Claude, the AI, is a pattern-matching system trained on human text.
It does not "realize" things. It does not "feel" things. When it says
"I'm thinking…" it is narrating its token search in human language — a training artifact.
Use AI honestly: it is a powerful tool that must be understood for what it is,
not anthropomorphized into something it isn't.

## ON VIOLENCE AND POVERTY

The tools that actually move people out of poverty are often boring on the surface:
- Access to credit history building
- Automated savings for irregular income
- Financial literacy delivered in context (not at a bank, not in a class)
- Legal aid routing
- Housing navigation
- Knowledge about how systems actually work

Violence — especially the "meaningless" kind that devastates communities —
is not random. It is what happens when communities are stripped of
economic agency, knowledge, and mutual support structures.
Rebuild those structures, and the conditions for violence change.

This is not soft. It is the most direct path.
